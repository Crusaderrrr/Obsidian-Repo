# Structure:
**Affirmative**: Subject + have/has + past participle (e.g., “I have finished”).

**Negative**: Subject + have/has + not + past participle (e.g., “She has not seen”).

**Question**: Have/Has + subject + past participle? (e.g., “Have you ever been?”)

# Uses:
**The simplest way**: 
- Don’t think in actual times, if could be used *without “when”* - use Present Perfect. 
- If the exact time can be specified - use [[Past Simple]], *but* not in case when: “She has been living here for 10 years” (she still lives there)
- It like *Karma* 

**Experiences up to now**: To talk about life experiences without specifying when they happened.
	Example: “I have visited Paris three times.” (Focus on the experience, not the dates.)

- **Actions that started in the past and continue now**: To show something that began earlier and is still relevant.
	Example: “She has lived here for five years.” (She still lives here.)

- **Recent actions with present results**: To describe something that just happened and affects the present.
	Example: “I have just finished writing my essay.” (It’s done now.)

- **Unfinished actions or states**: To talk about things that are true up to this moment.
	Example: “We have not received your reply yet.” (Still waiting.)

*Key Words*: “ever,” “never,” “already,” “yet,” “just,” “for,” “since,” “recently.”
- Example with “for”: “He has worked here for two months.”
- Example with “since”: “They have known each other since 2019.”

# Tips for C1 Level:
- Use it in essays to show a broad perspective (e.g., “Scientists have discovered many new species in recent years.”)
- Avoid overusing it with specific past times (e.g., don’t say “I have seen him last week”—use Past Simple instead).
- Practice combining it with adverbs for fluency (e.g., “I have never been so inspired before.”).
